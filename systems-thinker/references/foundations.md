# Foundations & Sources

Verified grounding for the concepts this skill uses. Read this when you want the precise origin of a model or need to cite it accurately. Each claim below is checked against its canonical source — don't overstate beyond what's here.

## The leverage hierarchy (why "mental models" is the deepest level)

Donella Meadows' **"Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System"** lists **twelve** places to intervene, ranked by increasing effectiveness. The shallow ones are parameters and numbers (subsidies, buffer sizes); the deep ones are **the goals of the system, the mindset/paradigm the system arises from, and — highest of all — the power to transcend paradigms.** This is the authority behind the **Find the leverage depth** lens's claim that *the belief holding a structure in place is often the highest-leverage and least-examined level*: changing a number rarely changes system behavior; changing the paradigm changes everything downstream.

> Source: Meadows, D. (1999). *Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System.* Sustainability Institute / The Donella Meadows Project. https://donellameadows.org/archives/leverage-points-places-to-intervene-in-a-system/ — also in *Thinking in Systems* (2008).

## The Iceberg Model (Events → Patterns → Structure → Mental Models)

Four progressively deeper levels: **Events** (the visible tip — what just happened), **Patterns/Trends** (what has recurred over time), **Structure** (the rules, incentives, and relationships generating the patterns), **Mental Models** (the beliefs and assumptions holding the structure in place). It is a *teaching tool from the systems-dynamics / organizational-learning tradition* — most associated with Peter Senge (*The Fifth Discipline*, 1990) and Daniel Kim's systems-thinking materials — **not a single-author invention.** Present it as a tradition, not as anyone's personal model. It pairs naturally with Meadows: the iceberg's bottom (mental models) maps to Meadows' top (paradigms).

> Source: Senge, P. (1990), *The Fifth Discipline*; Kim, D.H., *Systems Thinking Tools*. Summary: https://www.thinknpc.org/resource-hub/systems-practice-toolkit/the-iceberg-model/

## "Actants" (mapping human and non-human together)

In **Actor-Network Theory**, Bruno Latour uses **"actant"** (borrowed from semiotics) for *any* entity — human or non-human (technologies, documents, microbes, algorithms) — that exerts agency in a network. The term avoids "actor," which wrongly implies a human; ANT's **principle of symmetry** insists human and non-human be described in the same terms. This is why the **Map relationships and loops** lens treats technologies and policies as first-class participants, not background.

> Source: Latour, B. (2005), *Reassembling the Social: An Introduction to Actor-Network-Theory*, Oxford University Press.

## "You can never do merely one thing" (second-order effects)

Garrett Hardin's law of complex systems: any intervention propagates through interconnected variables and produces additional — often offsetting or counterproductive — effects beyond the intended one. This is the formal backing for the **Trace second-order effects** lens: assume your fix will do more than one thing, and trace what.

> Source: Hardin, G. (1972), *Exploring New Ethics for Survival: The Voyage of the Spaceship Beagle*, Viking Press.

## "Operation Cat Drop" — use carefully (high myth-risk)

The popular cautionary tale (DDT → poisoned insects → geckos → cats die by biomagnification → rats surge → plague; roofs collapse from caterpillars; **14,000** cats parachuted in) is a **popularized composite, and several details are disputed or exaggerated.**

**What is defensible:** In the 1950s the WHO sprayed DDT across Borneo to control malaria mosquitoes (well documented), and cats *were* genuinely airdropped into Sarawak around 1960 to help with a rat problem ("Operation Cat Drop"). **What is shaky:** the precise biomagnification chain, the roof-collapse story, the plague claim, and the famous "14,000 cats" figure (contemporary reports cite roughly **23**).

**Safe usage:** invoke it only as a *metaphor for tracing chains of unintended consequence* — never cite the embellished details as fact. If you mention it, keep it to "DDT spraying plus a real cat airdrop," and hedge the rest. The honest version is still a perfectly good illustration of the **Trace second-order effects** lens.

> Source: *Operation Cat Drop* — Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Cat_Drop

## Systems thinking as a recognized discipline (ISO 56001:2024)

Systems thinking isn't just a heuristic — it's named as one of the **eight guiding principles of ISO 56001:2024**, the first certifiable Innovation Management System standard. The standard defines an innovation management system as "a set of interrelated and interacting elements with the purpose of realizing value," which is the same relationships-not-elements stance behind the **Map relationships and loops** lens, and pairs systems thinking with adaptability and value realization. Useful when the **Push past the obvious** lens (the innovation filter) needs to connect to recognized practice rather than read as ad-hoc creativity.

> Source: ISO 56001:2024, *Innovation management system — Requirements*. https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/en/#!iso:std:79278:en
